AI Founders Are About to Get Sherlocked — and Most Don't See It Coming

Matt Rogers co-founded Nest, worked under Steve Jobs, and watched Apple kill startup after startup overnight. He's sounding the alarm: the same pattern is playing out in AI right now. The companies that survive won't have the best models — they'll be the ones that become operationally indispensable.

Key Takeaways

  • Apple's "Sherlocking" — absorbing a startup's entire reason to exist into the OS overnight — is now being replicated by OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google with every model release.
  • Products built as model wrappers or generic copilots are the most vulnerable. Small enough to bundle, no network effects to protect them.
  • Dropbox, Spotify, and 1Password all survived platform attacks by going deeper — becoming embedded in operations rather than fighting feature-for-feature.
  • The frontier labs build extraordinary models — but they cannot provide white-glove implementation for every hospital, law firm, and manufacturer on earth. That gap is the real opportunity.
  • The moat is operational integration: approvals, compliance, procurement, institutional knowledge. Once you're in, you're painful to remove.
By Matt Rogers (Co-founder, Nest & Mill)  ·  Fortune  ·  May 30, 2026

Snowflake & Databricks: What They Actually Do

You keep hearing these two names wherever enterprise AI is discussed. They're not AI companies — they're something more fundamental. Think of them as the plumbing and wiring that AI runs through. Without them, a company's data sits in dozens of disconnected silos, completely unusable by any AI system. With them, that same data becomes a living, queryable, AI-ready asset. Here's what each one actually does.

❄️ Snowflake
The Universal Filing Cabinet for Your Company's Data

Snowflake solved a problem that was quietly killing enterprise productivity for decades: a company's data was scattered across dozens of incompatible databases, and getting any two systems to talk to each other was an IT project that took months.

Snowflake puts all of it in one place — on any cloud (AWS, Azure, Google) — and brilliantly separates the storage (cheap, pay for what you keep) from the computing power (elastic, scale up only when you're actually querying). Any analyst in any department can now run SQL queries against the entire company's data, in seconds, from anywhere.

🔶 Databricks
The Engine Room for AI and Machine Learning at Scale

Databricks was built by the team that created Apache Spark at UC Berkeley — the processing engine that powers most of the world's large-scale data work. While Snowflake focused on structured data (rows, columns, SQL), Databricks focused on the messier stuff: unstructured data like documents, images, logs, and sensor feeds — exactly what AI models actually need to train on.

They invented the "data lakehouse" — combining the cheap, flexible storage of a data lake with the structure and queryability of a data warehouse. One platform for engineers, analysts, and data scientists to all work together.

🛢️ The Oil Refinery Analogy

Imagine AI models as cars — impressive, fast, everyone wants one. But cars are useless without gasoline. Your company's raw data is the crude oil sitting underground — potentially valuable, completely unrefined. Snowflake and Databricks are the refineries. They take that crude oil, clean it, process it, and turn it into something engines can actually run on.

The AI hype is about the cars. The real money — as Databricks' $134B valuation and Snowflake's +36% day this week both prove — is in the refineries.

Why This Matters Right Now

  • Every enterprise AI project starts here. Before a company can use Claude, GPT-4, or Gemini on their own data, they need Snowflake or Databricks (or both) to make that data accessible. They're the unsexy prerequisite that unlocks everything else.
  • They're converging. Snowflake is expanding into AI/ML (Cortex AI, just acquired Natoma for AI agent infrastructure). Databricks is expanding into SQL analytics. They're eating each other's territory — a sign both markets are enormous.
  • Snowflake just had its best stock day in company history (+36%) this week on AI-driven demand, while signing a $6 billion deal with AWS. Enterprises are opening the budget spigots for AI data infrastructure.
  • Databricks' IPO will be a market signal. If it prices at or above $134B, it validates the entire AI infrastructure sector and likely triggers a wave of similar listings.
  • For builders: if you're working with enterprise clients, the question isn't "do they have AI?" — it's "do they have their data house in order?" If not, that's your first conversation.

🎸 Dupree's Take

Read all three stories together and the picture is clear: the AI decade will be won at the infrastructure layer. Rogers says survive by becoming operationally embedded. Databricks and Snowflake prove it works — they're worth a combined $200B+ not because they have better AI, but because enterprises literally cannot function without them. The question for every builder right now isn't "what AI can I add?" — it's "which layer am I in, and how deep can I go before someone tries to Sherlock me?"
